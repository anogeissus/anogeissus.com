See What Your RAW Files Have Been Hiding

Your camera captured it. Your exports have been throwing it away.

Every RAW file you've ever shot holds up to four stops of real light your screen has never shown you — the glow of neon, sunlight inside a cloud, the shimmer of a white dress. It isn't lost. It's in the file, waiting for a screen that can show it. You probably own one already.

Your display is reporting high dynamic range. In each pair below, the second image's highlights should extend visibly brighter than the white of this page. Your display or browser is reporting standard dynamic range. The pairs below will look nearly identical here — the extra light is in the file, but this screen can't show it. Open this page on an M-series MacBook Pro or a recent iPhone, in Safari or Chrome.

Lights that actually glow

In every export you've ever made, nothing reads as switched on. A lit neon sign and the printed card beside it land on the same white, because SDR has nowhere brighter to put a light. In HDR the lamps and the neon rise above white and glow in their own color.

SDR. A window full of lit lamps, a neon sign and a glowing screen — and not one of them reads as lit.

HDR. Same RAW file. The lamps, the neon and the screen switch back on.

Snow and clouds that shine

Point a camera at a sunlit landscape and SDR files the cloud, the haze and the snow beneath them at the same value — the brightest third of the scene flattens into one white. In HDR the cloud is luminous again, the snow keeps its texture, and the depth comes back.

SDR. The cloud bank sitting on the range and the snow beneath it converge on the same white.

HDR. Same RAW file. The cloud shines, the snow keeps its texture, and the valley gets its depth back.

OM System OM-1, M.Zuiko Digital ED 12-45mm F4 Pro. Photograph © Shane Dias.

Whites that stay alive

A wedding dress in sunlight, a white shirt on a stage — SDR blows them out to a flat patch. In HDR the folds, the sheen and the embroidery are all still there. They were always in the file.

See it in your own files

  1. Open a RAW file in ApolloOne. On an HDR screen — any M-series MacBook Pro, or a recent iPhone or iPad with ApolloOne GO — the full range appears immediately. Nothing to switch on.
  2. Edit as you always do. Exposure, white balance, color. The extra stops stay live while you work.
  3. Export as HDR JPEG. One file: full brightness on HDR screens, a normal, correct photograph everywhere else. HEIC and JPEG XL are there too if you want them.
