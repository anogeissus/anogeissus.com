HDR browser test

Does your browser display HDR photographs? Two HDR formats and an SDR control.

How to read this page. On an HDR-capable screen the first two images should show highlights noticeably brighter than this page's white background. The third is an ordinary JPEG and should never do that — judge the other two against it.

If an image looks flat next to the control, your browser is showing its SDR base and ignoring the HDR data. That isn't a fault in the file: a gain-map JPEG is designed to degrade to a correct photograph exactly this way.
1 · Gain-map JPEG

JPEG with an ISO gain map and Ultra HDR signalling

Exported from ApolloOne 4.7.0. An ordinary JPEG carrying a gain map that describes, per pixel, how much brighter the HDR rendering should be, plus the signalling every current reader needs: Apple's AROT segment, the ISO gain map, and hdrgm with a Container:Directory on the primary image for Chrome. Declared headroom 8.20×.

One file that shows HDR where it can and a correct SDR photograph where it can't.

2 · AVIF

HDR AVIF in PQ — for reference

The same frame exported as AVIF with BT.2020 primaries and the PQ transfer function, signalled through CICP. No gain map and no SDR fallback, so a browser either understands PQ or it doesn't. Included as a second opinion: where both render, they should look close.

3 · SDR control

Ordinary JPEG

Plain sRGB, no HDR data of any kind. The baseline.

Known behaviour as of July 2026: Safari on macOS 15 Sequoia renders no HDR images in web pages at all; macOS 26 and iOS/iPadOS 26 render gain-map JPEG correctly. Chrome renders both formats, but only reads gain maps carrying the Ultra HDR signalling. Firefox renders neither.

How close 1 and 2 look depends on your display's headroom rather than on the formats. The further a display falls short of the file's declared range, the more the gain-map version trades highlight detail for brightness.
