HDR browser test

Does your browser display HDR photographs? Two HDR formats and an SDR control.

How to read this page. On an HDR-capable screen the first two images should show highlights noticeably brighter than this page's white background. The third is an ordinary JPEG and should never do that — judge the other two against it.

If an image looks flat next to the control, your browser is showing its SDR base and ignoring the HDR data. That isn't a fault in the file: a gain-map JPEG is designed to degrade to a correct photograph exactly this way.

On a Mac, the usual cause is the macOS version rather than the browser or the screen — see what your macOS version decides below.

How close 1 and 2 look depends on your display's headroom rather than on the formats. The further a display falls short of the file's declared range, the more the gain-map version trades highlight detail for brightness.

On a Mac, your macOS version decides this

If all three images looked alike on a Mac with an HDR-capable screen, the most likely reason is the operating system rather than the browser or the display.

Safari on Sequoia does not render HDR photographs in a web page at all — not gain-map JPEG, not AVIF, no matter how capable the display. It shows the SDR version and gives no indication there was anything more in the file. Tahoe fixed this, and every release since has kept it.

If you care about seeing photographs as they were made, this is a good reason to move to Tahoe. The HDR images on this page, and increasingly across the web, are carrying detail your current Safari is discarding before it reaches your eyes.

Staying on Sequoia for now? Chrome renders HDR correctly there, so you can see what you are missing without upgrading anything. On iPhone and iPad the current releases handle HDR properly and nothing needs changing. Firefox does not yet render HDR images on any platform.

Why this matters

A typical RAW file holds as much as four stops of highlight information above SDR white — real colour and real detail that a conventional export discards outright. Whether any of it reaches you depends on the format it was saved in and on the browser you are reading this in.
